Randy Savage and the Art of the Elbow Drop

There is a kind of genius that looks like madness from the outside. Randy Savage had it. The sequined robes, the sunglasses, the voice like a cement mixer full of gravel — all of it was intentional, considered, and completely his own.

Most people who watched wrestling in the eighties thought they were watching a cartoon. Savage knew they were watching theatre. He understood something that took the rest of the industry decades to catch up to: that the character had to be so fully realized, so internally consistent, that the audience would suspend disbelief not because they were fooled but because they wanted to be.
